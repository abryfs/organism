# North Star

## Who

Founder-engineers who build with AI. They can ship a product in a weekend. But shipping a product isn't building a company. They need to find users, tell a story, price it right, raise money, hire the first people, and know when to change course. AI made building cheap. Everything else is still hard — and founders face it alone.

## Pain

AI coding tools solved the build problem so completely that it exposed everything else. The founder can ship in a day, but:

- **They ship to nobody.** Distribution is exactly as hard as it always was. "First time founders focus on product. Second time founders focus on distribution." They build what they can control and avoid what's uncertain.
- **They can't explain what they built.** Technical founders describe features when users need benefits. They write for themselves, not their audience. The product works but the story doesn't land.
- **They don't know if the economics work.** They think in features, not unit economics. CAC, LTV, pricing, margins — these get figured out after launch, when they should be figured out before.
- **They build the wrong thing longer than they should.** When building costs near zero, the natural signal to stop ("this is too expensive") disappears. Founders can build forever without the market punishing them — until they run out of runway.
- **They react instead of focus.** A shiny feature catches their eye. A competitor launches something. A user requests something loud. AI makes it so easy to say yes that founders forget the most important word is no.
- **They fool themselves.** Confirmation bias, sunk cost fallacy, identity entanglement. "More startups die from founder ego than from competitors, bad markets, or running out of money."
- **They don't know when or who to hire.** When they finally need people, they don't know where to look, what to pay, or what culture they're building.

The tools they have (GSD, Superpowers, gstack) solve the build side: state tracking, TDD enforcement, browser QA. But they don't coordinate with each other, and none of them help with selling, funding, growing, hiring, or the founder's own blind spots.

## What Organism Does

Nine organs across three sides of the founder's job. Not just building the product — building the company.

### The Build Side (the product)

The coordination protocol that exists today. Four organs enforce discipline on what gets built:

- **Gut** — Is this the right thing? Demand research, competitive intelligence, user interviews, feedback processing. Every task checked against the focus before work begins.
- **Brain** — What's the plan? State tracking, phases, roadmap, lifecycle. Reads market context from Gut before planning.
- **Spine** — Is it solid? TDD enforcement, evidence requirements, quality gates. Blocks unverified claims and untestable plans.
- **Hands** — Does it work? Browser QA, visual verification, deployment. Tests what users actually see.

### The Business Side (the company)

New organs that help the founder do everything outside the codebase that determines whether the startup lives or dies:

- **Voice** — How do we tell the world? Marketing, positioning, copy, outreach emails, pitch decks, storytelling. Translates what was built into language that resonates with users, investors, and hires. Knows the difference between features and benefits.
- **Legs** — How do we find people? Distribution strategy, first users, growth channels, go-to-market. The organ that goes out there. Identifies where the target users already are, what channels work for this market, and how to get from 0 to 10 to 100 to 1000.
- **Blood** — How do we survive? Revenue path, pricing strategy, unit economics, financial modeling. Also fundraising: which investors, what terms, when to raise. The organ that keeps the organism alive. No blood, no organism.
- **Heart** — Who builds this with us? First hires, culture, values, team structure, compensation. The kind of company the founder is building. Not just finding people — defining what they're joining.

### The Founder Side (the person)

The organ the research says is the biggest gap nobody addresses:

- **Eyes** — What do we see clearly? Two directions:
  - **Outward:** Scanning for specific people — investors, advisors, hires, partners. Finding warm paths. Identifying opportunities — grants, accelerators, partnerships.
  - **Inward:** The founder's own biases and blind spots. "Are you building this because users need it, or because you enjoy building it?" Cognitive bias detection, pivot/persevere/quit timing, pattern reflection. Challenges reasoning when it goes circular. Surfaces what the founder doesn't want to see.

### The Pitfall Layer

Every organ carries the documented history of failures in its domain. Not just advice — the specific, named patterns that have killed startups, lost markets, burned founders, and wasted years. Each organ validates the founder's decisions against these patterns before acting.

- **Gut** checks: "You're building for a persona you invented, not one you interviewed." / "The demand signal is your own excitement, not market evidence." / "You're ignoring disconfirming feedback for the third time."
- **Brain** checks: "You estimated 3 days. Features of this type have taken 3x in this codebase." / "You're planning infrastructure before validating the monkey." / "Scope expanded 40% since the plan — classic second-system effect."
- **Spine** checks: "You wrote code before the test — again." / "You're patching symptoms, not root causes. 3 failed fixes = architectural problem." / "This 'done' has no evidence attached."
- **Hands** checks: "You didn't test on mobile." / "The error state shows a stack trace to the user." / "The happy path works but 3 edge cases are untested."
- **Voice** checks: "You're describing features, not benefits — the #1 marketing mistake technical founders make." / "This pitch buries the problem and leads with the solution." / "Your cold outreach reads like AI wrote it."
- **Legs** checks: "You're launching on ProductHunt before you have 10 users who love it." / "You're building a growth engine before you have product-market fit." / "Your distribution plan requires channels you haven't validated."
- **Blood** checks: "You're pricing based on cost, not value." / "Your CAC exceeds your LTV — you're paying people to be customers." / "You're raising before you have leverage. The terms will crush you."
- **Heart** checks: "You're hiring to delegate discomfort, not to fill a capability gap." / "You're offering too much equity too early." / "You don't know what culture you're building — you're just hiring friends."
- **Eyes** checks: "You've pivoted 3 times in 2 weeks — that's not iterating, that's avoiding commitment." / "You're ignoring the same data point for the fourth time." / "You're building because building feels productive. Selling feels scary. That's the tell."

The pitfall layer is the organism's immune system. Antibodies for every known failure pattern. It doesn't block the founder — it surfaces the pattern, names it, and lets the founder decide. But it never stays silent when it recognizes a pattern.

### How They Work Together

The build side has a coordination protocol: Gut → Brain → Spine → Hands → verify → reality → health check. The business and founder organs participate in this protocol at the steps where they're relevant:

- Voice contributes to Gut reality checks ("will the persona understand this?") and produces outward-facing materials after Hands ships.
- Legs informs Brain's planning ("build the thing users can find, not the thing that's technically interesting").
- Blood gates scope ("is this feature worth building at this unit cost?") and runs alongside Brain for fundraising tasks.
- Heart surfaces when the founder's capacity is the bottleneck ("you need help — here's what role to hire first").
- Eyes runs continuously. Outward scanning feeds Gut and Blood. Inward reflection feeds every health check.
- Every organ runs its pitfall checks on every decision in its domain. Patterns get named, not just flagged.

## One Metric

Does the founder build the right thing, sell it to real people, price it to survive, and know when to change course — with evidence at every step? Not lines of code. Not speed. Ownership of the company, not just the product.

## Anti-Goals

- Not a framework. No runtime dependencies, no build step, no lock-in.
- Not a replacement for GSD, Superpowers, or gstack. The nervous system that coordinates them — and now coordinates business tools too.
- Not autonomous. The founder decides. The organism enforces discipline, surfaces information, and does the research. The founder acts on it.
- Not another vibe coding accelerator. Slower where it matters (thinking, grounding, saying no), faster where it doesn't (execution, research, outreach drafts).
- Not locked to any system. Open to any tool that makes part of the founder's job better.
- Not a business consultant. Researches, drafts, prepares, and calculates. Doesn't make business decisions — gives the founder the evidence to make them.
